# Preface

> _The ancient Greeks left us with many other questions, such as "What is the nature of justice?", "What is a good society?", "What are the reasonable purposes and goals of human life?", "What is the nature of language and meaning?", "What is the nature of truth?" etc., which remain philosophical questions for us today._
>
> — _John Searle, The Future of Philosophy_

> Philosophy is the study of big questions. No matter how it separates, as long as the separated disciplines face big questions, they will still become philosophical questions. From the perspective of big questions, first, no matter how the branches of philosophy separate, the big questions themselves will not be separated. As Wilson said in "Philosophy in Brief": Philosophy is not individual concepts, such as alimony, deflation, perspective, but universal concepts, such as causality, knowledge, freedom. People can do without individual concepts, but cannot live without universal concepts. His work on philosophical principles is composed of seven major concepts (science, spirit, freedom, knowledge, language, objectivity, God). More importantly, philosophical questions as big questions lie in the fact that they can never be solved. Therefore, science wants to occupy the achievements obtained by philosophy, so let it occupy; what is scientific will one day be occupied by science; what is not scientific, whenever it is, belongs to philosophy. Therefore, Salvador said: "Some questions can be satisfactorily answered, and these are the questions that science deals with; other questions we think can never be thoroughly solved. Answering them in a way that can never be entirely satisfactory is the task of philosophy." Here "never can be thoroughly solved" hits the point.
>
> — _Zhang Fa, 2010, Jianghan University Journal_
